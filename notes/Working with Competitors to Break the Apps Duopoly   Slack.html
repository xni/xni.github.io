Working with Competitors to Break the Apps Duopoly

HTML5 Everywhere

The web is the biggest collection of digital content that exists. There are more web developers than there are developers for any flavour of native app. The creators of every single mobile operating system on the market today promote HTML5 (a web technology) as a way to create apps for their OS (see the links below):

  • Android
  • iOS
  • Windows Phone
  • Blackberry OS
  • Fire OS
  • Tizen
  • WebOS
  • Ubuntu Phone
  • Sailfish OS

Fragmentation

Despite supporting HTML5 apps, alternative mobile operating systems like Firefox OS, Tizen and Ubuntu Phone are consistently criticised in the tech press as being fundamentally flawed due to the lack of apps in their app stores. Why is this?

The reason is that although all of the platforms we share the "Other" category with in market share pie charts technically support HTML5 apps, these apps are severely fragmented. Each of these operating systems (Firefox OS included) tries to imitate the app store model of iOS and Android where developers submit their apps to a single central app store. In order to do this, each operating system has invented their own manifest file format, and their own packaging format. This means that every upstart mobile operating system is starting from scratch, and faces an insurmountable chicken and egg problem.

Nobody will create apps for a platform with no users, and nobody will use a platform with no apps.

This has allowed Android and iOS to maintain a duopoly in the mobile operating system market which not even big players like Microsoft and Blackberry have been able to overcome.

Emerging Web Standards

Until recently there hasn't been a standard for web apps which multiple vendors were able to agree on. But after a couple of false starts, the W3C now has a proposed Manifest for web application specification which has already been implemented by Google in Chrome for Android, is targeted for Firefox OS 2.2 by Mozilla and also has some support from Microsoft and Apple.

This specification is the first piece of the puzzle for a web app standard and other specifications like Service Workers and WebRTC solve more pieces of that puzzle.

What if all of those "alternative" operating systems pooled their apps together? What if the web itself was the app store and OS vendors just maintained their own curated directory of those apps?

Web manifests are crawlable, so anyone is free to crawl the web to generate their own app index. A web app created for one OS is instantly useful for another OS. Users are free to move their personal collection of apps from one OS to another.

This could finally crack the chicken and egg problem because if an app created for the web can automatically become discoverable and usable on multiple operating systems (including Android) without modification, the web suddenly becomes a more attractive target for app developers. As the number of apps increase, users will also be attracted to alternative operating systems, in the knowledge that they can freely move their apps to another OS if things don't work out.

Let's make the question of "how many apps are available for your OS?" as ridiculous as asking "how many web sites are available in your browser?".

Evangelism

Paradoxically I believe the best way to gain significant market share for Firefox OS is to help its competitors. If we can convince those alternative operating systems, and even big players like Microsoft and Blackberry, to adopt a common standard for web apps then we can all overcome the chicken and egg problem together and finally enjoy a level playing field. Once the flood gates of the open web are opened, there will be no closing them again.

Mozilla is uniquely positioned to do this due to our public interest mission. None of the other players will initiate this, but all of them stand to benefit.

I believe the route to unlocking market share for Firefox OS is through radical evangelism of web standards in a way we've never done before.

We should literally be knocking on the door of every one of the "Other" operating system makers and convincing them that the only way they stand a chance of loosening the grip of Apple and Google is to co-operate. Not for some liberal hippy charitable cause, but for measurable increases in market share. They can stop fighting the uphill struggle to bootstrap an app store, and start competing on the quality of their products.

We should be actively encouraging developers to make apps for competing operating systems by targeting the web as a whole, not just Firefox OS. Only then will there be a level playing field on which Firefox OS has the opportunity to lead.

What could be more central to our mission of promoting openness, innovation and opportunity on the Web?
